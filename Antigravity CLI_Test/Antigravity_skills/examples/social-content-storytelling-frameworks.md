# Social Content Storytelling Frameworks

**Purpose:** This document is a synthesized reference of storytelling and content structure frameworks distilled from expert creator analysis. It is designed to be loaded as a reference asset inside a Social Content Creator skill, enabling Claude to apply proven narrative techniques when generating platform-specific social content.

## How to Use This Document

When creating social media content, apply frameworks in this order:

1. **Choose a Narrative Architecture** (Section 1) — decide the macro shape of your content
2. **Apply Moment-Level Craft** (Section 2) — make each scene vivid and immersive
3. **Engineer the Entry Point** (Section 3) — design the hook that earns attention
4. **Design the Retention Engine** (Section 4) — build mechanisms that keep people watching
5. **Calibrate Delivery Texture** (Section 5) — match rhythm, tone, and format to platform
6. **Differentiate with Angle Selection** (Section 6) — ensure the content has a unique perspective

## 1. Narrative Architectures

> These are the macro structures for how a piece of content is shaped from beginning to end. Select one per piece of content.

### 1A. The Tension Dance

Content alternates between **setup beats** and **disruption beats** throughout. Rather than building linearly, the narrative weaves between giving the audience context and then undercutting or complicating that context.

The connective tissue between each beat should be causal, not sequential. Test this by checking: can you replace "and then" between any two beats with "but" or "therefore"? If every transition is "and then," the content is flat. Causal connectors ("but," "therefore," "which meant that," "except") create forward pull because each beat changes the trajectory.

**When to use:** Content about journeys, case studies, process reveals, behind-the-scenes stories. Works especially well for anything with twists or unexpected learnings.

**Structure:**

```text
Setup -> Disruption -> New Setup -> Deeper Disruption -> Resolution

```

**Application example (LinkedIn post about a failed launch):**

```text
Beat 1 (Setup): We spent 4 months building the perfect feature.
Beat 2 (Disruption — "but"): But on launch day, exactly zero people used it.
Beat 3 (New Setup — "therefore"): So we pulled the entire team into a war room.
Beat 4 (Deeper Disruption — "but"): But the problem wasn't the feature. It was that we'd never talked to a single customer.
Beat 5 (Resolution — "therefore"): That week I made a rule: no build starts without 10 customer conversations.

```

### 1B. The Transformation Arc

Content follows a clear before -> process -> after structure. The power comes from **the contrast gap** between the starting point and the end point — the wider the gap, the more compelling the content.

**When to use:** Product showcases, personal growth stories, tutorial outcomes, redesigns, makeovers, before/after comparisons. High-performing on visual platforms (Instagram, TikTok, YouTube Shorts).

**Structure:**

```text
Starting State -> Process/Action -> Transformed State

```

**Key principle:** The starting state must be specific enough to be relatable ("my engagement was stuck at 2% for six months") rather than vague ("things weren't going well"). The transformed state must be concrete and verifiable.

### 1C. The Subverted Expectation

Content deliberately builds a predictable trajectory — then pivots. The audience is unconsciously predicting the ending the entire time they consume the content. When the actual conclusion departs from that prediction, it creates an emotional spike (surprise, relief, humor, relatability) that drives shares and comments.

**When to use:** Personal stories, vulnerability-driven content, contrarian takes, "what I actually learned" posts. Especially effective for content about failure, pivots, or counterintuitive lessons.

**Structure:**

```text
Establish Trajectory -> Build Momentum Along Expected Path -> Pivot to Unexpected Conclusion

```

**Key principle:** The pivot must still feel earned. It shouldn't feel random — it should recontextualize everything. The audience should think "I didn't see that coming, but it makes perfect sense."

**Application example (Instagram carousel about career growth):**

```text
Slide 1-3: Year 1 = small office. Year 2 = bigger office. Year 3 = dream corner office.
Slide 4 (the pivot): Year 4 = back to my kitchen table. And it was the best decision.
Slide 5: Sometimes growth means going back to what actually works.

```
